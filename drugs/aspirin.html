Aspirin

Also known as: Acetylsalicylic acid, Disprin, Ecosprin

Pain Relief Heart Protection Anti-inflammatory

Aspirin is a widely used medication for pain relief, fever reduction, and importantly, for preventing heart attacks and strokes. It works by reducing inflammation and preventing blood clots.

Overview

Drug Class:
NSAID (Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drug) and Antiplatelet agent
Available as:
Tablets, dispersible tablets, enteric-coated tablets, suppositories
Prescription:
Available over-the-counter for pain relief; prescription for cardiovascular protection
Common Brands in India:
Disprin, Ecosprin, Loprin, Delisprin, Aspro

Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) is one of the oldest and most widely used medications in the world. It belongs to a class of drugs called NSAIDs and has unique antiplatelet properties that make it valuable for preventing cardiovascular events.

Aspirin works by irreversibly inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, which reduces the production of prostaglandins responsible for pain, inflammation, and fever. Its antiplatelet effect helps prevent blood clots by reducing platelet aggregation.

Key Points

  • Dual action: pain relief and cardiovascular protection
  • Low-dose aspirin is used for heart attack and stroke prevention
  • Should not be given to children under 16 due to Reye's syndrome risk
  • Can cause stomach irritation and bleeding
  • Requires careful monitoring in elderly patients

Uses

Aspirin has multiple therapeutic uses, ranging from pain relief to cardiovascular protection. The dose and duration vary significantly depending on the intended use.

Cardiovascular Protection

Low-dose aspirin for preventing heart attacks and strokes in high-risk patients.

Pain Relief

Effective for headaches, muscle pain, and general aches and pains.

Fever Reduction

Reduces body temperature in febrile conditions.

Anti-inflammatory

Reduces inflammation in conditions like arthritis and rheumatic disorders.

Blood Clot Prevention

Prevents formation of blood clots in arteries and veins.

Stroke Prevention

Reduces risk of ischemic stroke in appropriate patients.

Important Warning

Reye's Syndrome Risk: Aspirin should not be given to children and teenagers under 16 years of age during viral infections due to the risk of Reye's syndrome, a rare but serious condition affecting the liver and brain.

Dosage

Aspirin dosing varies significantly based on the intended use. Cardiovascular protection requires much lower doses than pain relief.

Dosage Guidelines by Indication

Indication Dose Frequency Maximum Daily Dose
Pain relief (adults) 300-600 mg Every 4 hours as needed 4000 mg (4 g)
Cardiovascular protection 75-100 mg Once daily 100 mg
Anti-inflammatory 500-1000 mg Every 4-6 hours 4000 mg (4 g)
Acute coronary syndrome 150-300 mg initial, then 75-100 mg Once daily maintenance As prescribed

Important Dosage Warnings

  • Do not exceed the stated dose without medical supervision
  • Take with food or milk to reduce stomach irritation
  • Enteric-coated tablets should be swallowed whole
  • For cardiovascular protection, consistency in daily dosing is crucial
  • Do not stop cardiovascular aspirin suddenly without consulting your doctor

Special Populations

Elderly: Increased risk of bleeding; may require dose reduction and closer monitoring.

Kidney/Liver Impairment: Use with caution; dose adjustment may be necessary.

Pregnancy: Avoid in third trimester; use only if benefits outweigh risks in first two trimesters.

Children: Contraindicated under 16 years due to Reye's syndrome risk.

Side Effects

Aspirin can cause various side effects, particularly affecting the gastrointestinal system and bleeding risk. The risk increases with higher doses and longer duration of use.

Common Side Effects

  • Stomach upset, nausea
  • Heartburn, indigestion
  • Mild stomach pain
  • Easy bruising

Less Common Side Effects

  • Dizziness, headache
  • Ringing in ears (tinnitus)
  • Skin rash or itching
  • Prolonged bleeding from cuts

Serious Side Effects (seek immediate medical attention)

  • Stomach or intestinal bleeding (black, tarry stools; vomiting blood)
  • Severe allergic reactions (difficulty breathing, swelling)
  • Signs of liver problems (yellowing of skin/eyes)
  • Unusual bleeding or bruising
  • Severe stomach pain
  • Hearing problems or severe tinnitus

Bleeding Risk Warning

Aspirin increases bleeding risk throughout the body. This includes:

  • Gastrointestinal bleeding (stomach, intestines)
  • Increased bleeding during surgery or dental procedures
  • Easy bruising and prolonged bleeding from minor cuts
  • Rare but serious: brain bleeding (hemorrhagic stroke)

Seek immediate medical attention for any signs of serious bleeding.

Precautions

Several important precautions must be observed when taking aspirin, particularly regarding bleeding risk and drug interactions.

Stomach Protection

Take with food or milk. Consider proton pump inhibitors for long-term use.

Bleeding Risk

Monitor for signs of bleeding. Inform healthcare providers before procedures.

Age Restrictions

Not for children under 16 years due to Reye's syndrome risk.

Alcohol

Limit alcohol consumption as it increases bleeding and stomach irritation risk.

Surgery

May need to stop before surgery. Consult your doctor about timing.

Allergies

Avoid if allergic to aspirin or other NSAIDs. Watch for asthma worsening.

Duration of Use

For pain relief: Do not use for more than 10 days without consulting a healthcare professional.

For cardiovascular protection: Usually taken long-term under medical supervision with regular monitoring.

Interactions

Aspirin interacts with numerous medications, particularly those affecting bleeding risk and blood sugar control.

Medication/Substance Interaction Recommendation
Warfarin and other anticoagulants Significantly increased bleeding risk Requires careful monitoring and dose adjustment
Other NSAIDs (ibuprofen, diclofenac) Increased risk of stomach bleeding and kidney problems Avoid combination unless specifically prescribed
Methotrexate Increased methotrexate toxicity Monitor blood counts closely
Diabetes medications Enhanced blood sugar lowering effect Monitor blood glucose levels
ACE inhibitors Reduced effectiveness of blood pressure medication Monitor blood pressure
Alcohol Increased risk of stomach bleeding Limit alcohol consumption
Corticosteroids Increased risk of stomach ulcers Consider stomach protection
Lithium Increased lithium levels Monitor lithium blood levels

Note: This is not a complete list. Always inform your healthcare provider about all medications, supplements, and herbal products you are taking.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I take aspirin for heart protection?

Low-dose aspirin for cardiovascular protection should only be taken under medical supervision. Your doctor will assess your individual risk factors, including age, blood pressure, cholesterol levels, and bleeding risk, to determine if aspirin is appropriate for you.

Can I take aspirin with other pain relievers?

Aspirin can be taken with paracetamol, but should generally not be combined with other NSAIDs like ibuprofen or diclofenac due to increased risk of side effects. Always consult your pharmacist or doctor before combining pain medications.

Why can't children take aspirin?

Aspirin is associated with Reye's syndrome in children and teenagers under 16, especially during viral infections like flu or chickenpox. Reye's syndrome is a rare but serious condition that affects the liver and brain. Paracetamol or ibuprofen are safer alternatives for children.

What should I do if I miss a dose of my daily aspirin?

If you miss a dose of daily aspirin for cardiovascular protection, take it as soon as you remember on the same day. If it's almost time for your next dose, skip the missed dose and continue with your regular schedule. Do not take a double dose to make up for a missed one.

Should I stop aspirin before surgery or dental work?

This depends on your individual situation and the type of procedure. For major surgery, aspirin is often stopped 7-10 days beforehand to reduce bleeding risk. However, for cardiovascular protection, the decision to stop aspirin must balance bleeding risk against the risk of heart attack or stroke. Always discuss this with both your surgeon and the doctor who prescribed your aspirin.

What's the difference between regular and enteric-coated aspirin?

Enteric-coated aspirin has a special coating that prevents the tablet from dissolving in the stomach, instead releasing the medication in the small intestine. This may reduce stomach irritation, though the evidence for this benefit is limited. Enteric-coated tablets should be swallowed whole and not crushed or chewed.
