Antidiabetic (Biguanide)

Metformin

Also known as: Glucophage, Glycomet, Obimet, Formet

Diabetes Blood Sugar Control PCOS

Metformin is the first-line medication for type 2 diabetes. It helps control blood sugar levels by improving insulin sensitivity and reducing glucose production by the liver.

Overview

Drug Class:
Biguanide antidiabetic agent
Available as:
Immediate-release tablets, extended-release tablets, oral solution
Prescription:
Prescription only medicine
Common Brands in India:
Glucophage, Glycomet, Obimet, Formet, Metsmall

Metformin is a biguanide antidiabetic medication that is the first-line treatment for type 2 diabetes mellitus. It has been used safely for over 60 years and is recommended by all major diabetes guidelines worldwide.

Metformin works primarily by decreasing hepatic glucose production, improving insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues, and slightly delaying glucose absorption from the intestines. Unlike some other diabetes medications, metformin does not cause hypoglycemia when used alone.

Key Points

  • First-line treatment for type 2 diabetes
  • Does not cause hypoglycemia when used alone
  • May help with weight loss or weight maintenance
  • Also used for PCOS and prediabetes
  • Generally well-tolerated with proper use

Uses

Metformin has several approved and off-label uses, primarily related to glucose metabolism and insulin resistance.

Type 2 Diabetes

Primary indication for controlling blood glucose levels in type 2 diabetes mellitus.

PCOS

Helps improve insulin resistance and may restore ovulation in polycystic ovary syndrome.

Prediabetes

May help prevent progression from prediabetes to type 2 diabetes.

Weight Management

May assist with weight loss or prevent weight gain in diabetes patients.

Cardiovascular Protection

May provide cardiovascular benefits in patients with diabetes.

Gestational Diabetes

Sometimes used during pregnancy for gestational diabetes management.

Note: Metformin is not effective for type 1 diabetes as it requires some insulin production by the pancreas to work effectively.

Dosage

Metformin dosing is individualized based on effectiveness and tolerability. It's typically started at a low dose and gradually increased to minimize gastrointestinal side effects.

Standard Dosage Guidelines

Indication Starting Dose Maintenance Dose Maximum Dose
Type 2 Diabetes (Adults) 500 mg twice daily with meals 1000 mg twice daily 2550 mg daily
Type 2 Diabetes (Children ≥10 years) 500 mg twice daily with meals 1000 mg twice daily 2000 mg daily
PCOS 500 mg once or twice daily 1500-2000 mg daily 2000 mg daily
Prediabetes 500 mg twice daily 850 mg twice daily 1700 mg daily

Important Dosage Guidelines

  • Always take with meals to reduce stomach upset
  • Increase dose gradually every 1-2 weeks as tolerated
  • Extended-release formulations are taken once daily with evening meal
  • Dose reduction required in kidney impairment
  • Do not crush or chew extended-release tablets

Special Populations

Elderly: Start with lower doses and monitor kidney function closely.

Kidney Impairment: Contraindicated if eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m²; dose reduction if eGFR 30-45.

Liver Impairment: Use with caution; contraindicated in severe liver disease.

Pregnancy: Generally considered safe; often continued during pregnancy for diabetes management.

Side Effects

Metformin is generally well-tolerated, but gastrointestinal side effects are common, especially when starting treatment or increasing doses.

Very Common Side Effects (>10%)

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Abdominal pain
  • Loss of appetite
  • Metallic taste in mouth

Common Side Effects (1-10%)

  • Flatulence (gas)
  • Indigestion
  • Weakness or fatigue
  • Headache
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency (with long-term use)

Rare but Serious Side Effects

  • Lactic acidosis (potentially fatal)
  • Severe allergic reactions
  • Liver problems
  • Severe vitamin B12 deficiency leading to anemia

Lactic Acidosis Warning

Lactic acidosis is a rare but serious complication that can occur with metformin use. Risk factors include:

  • Kidney disease
  • Liver disease
  • Heart failure
  • Severe infection or dehydration
  • Excessive alcohol use

Symptoms include: muscle pain, difficulty breathing, stomach pain, dizziness, feeling cold, slow heart rate.

Seek immediate medical attention if these symptoms occur.

Precautions

Several important precautions must be observed when taking metformin, particularly regarding kidney function and situations that increase lactic acidosis risk.

Kidney Function

Regular monitoring required. Contraindicated in severe kidney disease.

Take with Food

Always take with meals to reduce gastrointestinal side effects.

Alcohol

Limit alcohol intake as it increases lactic acidosis risk.

Contrast Procedures

Stop before contrast imaging procedures and restart after 48 hours.

Surgery

May need to be stopped before major surgery. Consult your doctor.

Illness

Stop during severe illness, dehydration, or fever and consult doctor.

Monitoring Requirements

Regular monitoring needed for:

  • Blood glucose levels
  • Kidney function (creatinine, eGFR)
  • Vitamin B12 levels (annually with long-term use)
  • Liver function tests

Interactions

Metformin has relatively few drug interactions, but some medications can affect its effectiveness or increase the risk of side effects.

Medication/Substance Interaction Recommendation
Contrast agents (iodinated) Increased risk of lactic acidosis Stop metformin before procedure, restart after 48 hours
Alcohol Increased risk of lactic acidosis Limit alcohol consumption
Cimetidine Increased metformin levels Monitor for metformin side effects
Furosemide Increased metformin levels Monitor blood glucose and metformin effects
Corticosteroids May increase blood glucose Monitor blood glucose more frequently
Thiazide diuretics May increase blood glucose Monitor blood glucose levels
Insulin/Sulfonylureas Increased risk of hypoglycemia Monitor blood glucose; may need dose adjustment
ACE inhibitors May enhance glucose-lowering effect Monitor blood glucose levels

Note: Always inform your healthcare provider about all medications, supplements, and herbal products you are taking before starting metformin.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will metformin help me lose weight?

Metformin may help with modest weight loss or prevent weight gain, but it's not primarily a weight loss medication. The weight effects are usually small (2-3 kg on average) and occur mainly in people with diabetes or insulin resistance. It should not be used solely for weight loss in people without diabetes.

Can I take metformin if I have kidney problems?

Metformin use depends on the severity of kidney problems. It's contraindicated if your eGFR is below 30 mL/min/1.73m². If your eGFR is between 30-45, the dose may need to be reduced. Regular kidney function monitoring is essential for all patients taking metformin.

Why do I need to stop metformin before certain medical procedures?

Metformin is stopped before procedures involving iodinated contrast agents (like CT scans with contrast) because the contrast can temporarily affect kidney function, increasing the risk of lactic acidosis. It's typically stopped 48 hours before the procedure and restarted 48 hours after, once kidney function is confirmed to be normal.

What should I do if I experience stomach problems with metformin?

Stomach problems are common when starting metformin. Take it with meals, start with a low dose, and increase gradually. Extended-release formulations may cause fewer stomach problems. If problems persist, consult your doctor - they may adjust your dose or suggest alternative formulations.

Is it safe to take metformin during pregnancy?

Metformin is generally considered safe during pregnancy and is often used for gestational diabetes and in women with PCOS. It doesn't cross the placenta significantly and may reduce the risk of pregnancy complications. However, always consult your doctor about continuing or starting metformin during pregnancy.

How long does it take for metformin to work?

Metformin starts working within a few days, but it may take 2-3 weeks to see the full effect on blood glucose levels. For PCOS, it may take 2-3 months to see improvements in menstrual cycles. Your doctor will monitor your response and may adjust the dose accordingly.
